**Paper:** L4P: Towards Unified Low-Level 4D Vision Perception, arXiv 2025, L4P
**Summary:** L4P is a feed-forward RGB video model that combines a pretrained VideoMAE encoder with lightweight task heads for dense depth, optical flow, motion segmentation, camera rays/poses, and sparse 2D/3D point tracking. It processes fixed 16-frame clips at inference, uses overlapping-window alignment or tracking memory for longer videos, and is trained mainly with synthetic supervision while requiring no ground-truth camera parameters at inference.

1. **3D reconstruction:** Yes. L4P supports 3D/4D reconstruction by estimating dense depth, camera pose/rays, and 3D tracks that can be placed in a common reference frame, but its primary dense output is depth plus estimated camera geometry rather than a native DUSt3R-style pointmap; the supplement also describes lifting depth maps to 3D point clouds for pose-window alignment (Section 3.2, page 4; Figure 5, page 8; Supplementary Section 6, supplementary page 4; Supplementary Figure 7, supplementary page 3).
2. **Dense reconstruction:** Yes. Dense prediction is central to the method: the DPT-style heads output dense depth, optical flow, motion segmentation, and camera rays, while sparse point tracking is an additional head rather than the only output mode (Section 3.2, page 4).
3. **World-space output:** Yes. L4P can put depth, camera trajectories, and 3D point tracks into a shared reference system using estimated camera geometry and scale-consistent training/alignment, as shown in the main tracking/pose visualization and supplementary 4D reconstruction results (Figure 5, page 8; Supplementary Section 4, supplementary page 2; Supplementary Figure 7, supplementary page 3).
4. **Camera-space output (egocentric):** Yes. The method predicts per-frame depth maps and, for a queried track, per-frame track depth with respect to the camera, so camera-relative geometry is directly represented before any common-frame visualization (Section 3.2, page 4; Section 3.3, pages 4-5).
5. **Flexible camera frame:** Partial. L4P can transform predictions into a common reference frame using estimated poses and align longer-window predictions with 3D similarity transforms, but the paper does not describe a user-selectable arbitrary reference-camera interface (Figure 5, page 8; Supplementary Section 6, supplementary page 4).
6. **Metric scale:** No. The paper explicitly states that estimated depth maps have inherent scale ambiguity and evaluates depth after linear alignment to ground truth; TAPVid-3D evaluation similarly uses global median scaling for track depth, so L4P is scale-consistent internally but not demonstrated as absolute metric scale (Section 5.1, page 6; Section 5.3, page 7; Supplementary Section 4, supplementary page 2).
7. **Point tracking:** Yes. L4P tracks queried pixels through video by predicting their corresponding locations, depth, and visibility over time, and it is evaluated on TAPVid-3D tracking benchmarks (Section 3.3, pages 4-5; Section 5.3, page 7).
8. **3D tracks:** Yes. The sparse head defines a 3D trajectory as 2D location, depth, and visibility for each frame, and the paper reports 3D tracking results on TAPVid-3D (Section 3.3, pages 4-5; Table 4, page 7).
9. **2D tracks:** Yes. L4P directly predicts 2D point tracks using per-frame probability heatmaps and soft-argmax, and the paper reports 2D tracking metrics on TAPVid-3D (Section 3.3.1, page 5; Table 3, page 7).
10. **Occlusion / visibility prediction:** Yes. The tracking head includes a visibility token and predicts whether each track is visible or occluded at each frame; the evaluation also reports occlusion accuracy (Section 3.3, pages 4-5; Section 5.3, page 7).
11. **Multiple views:** No. L4P is formulated around an RGB video clip processed by a VideoMAE encoder with fixed temporal windows, and longer inputs are handled as longer temporal videos via overlapping windows or tracking memory; the paper does not present simultaneous multi-camera/multi-view images as a supported input mode (Section 3, page 3; Section 3.2, page 4; Section 3.3.2, page 5).
12. **Global context vs. pairwise vs. fixed-window:** Hybrid. The base model has fixed-window inference because VideoMAE processes 16-frame clips, dense long-video inference uses overlapping windows with task-specific alignment or overwriting, pose uses 3D similarity alignment across windows, and sparse tracking adds an online memory mechanism for longer sequences (Section 3.2, page 4; Section 3.3.2, page 5; Supplementary Section 6, supplementary page 4).
13. **No GT extrinsics required at inference:** Yes. Inference is from RGB video clips and optional point prompts, while ground-truth extrinsics are used for training annotations and evaluation rather than being required model inputs at inference (Section 3, page 3; Section 4, page 5; Supplementary Section 6, supplementary page 4).
14. **Extrinsics as output:** Yes. L4P includes a camera pose estimation head that predicts dense Plucker ray bundles and recovers camera centers, rotations, and pose representations through least-squares optimization, with pose benchmarks reported on Sintel, TUM-dynamics, and ScanNet (Section 5.5, page 8; Supplementary Section 6, supplementary page 4; Supplementary Table 2, supplementary page 3).
15. **No GT intrinsics required at inference:** Yes. The supplementary pose evaluation explicitly considers inference both with and without camera intrinsics and states that the Plucker-ray approach works in either setting (Supplementary Table 2, supplementary page 3; Supplementary Section 6, supplementary page 4).
16. **Intrinsics as output:** Partial. The paper says camera intrinsics can be recovered from estimated ray bundles and uses estimated intrinsics for 4D reconstruction visualizations, but it does not provide a dedicated intrinsics-estimation benchmark or metric (Section 5.5, page 8; Supplementary Section 7, supplementary page 4).
17. **Moving cameras:** Yes. L4P is designed and evaluated on video sequences with moving cameras, including depth, pose, and tracking experiments on datasets such as Sintel, TUM-dynamics, ScanNet, KITTI, TAPVid-3D, and DriveTrack (Section 5.1, page 6; Section 5.3, page 7; Section 5.5, page 8).
18. **Dynamic content:** Yes. Dynamic content is central to the paper: the model handles point tracking through occlusions, motion-based segmentation, dynamic-scene depth, and pose evaluation on dynamic datasets such as TUM-dynamics and TAPVid-3D subsets (Section 3.3, pages 4-5; Section 5.3, page 7; Section 5.5, page 8).
19. **Feed-forward inference:** Yes. The paper repeatedly describes L4P as feed-forward and reports roughly 300 ms for a 16-frame 224 by 224 clip, or about 28 ms per frame when including overlapping-window overhead for long videos (Abstract, page 1; Section 4, page 5; Supplementary Section 1, supplementary page 1).

**Notes:** L4P is an RGB-video model centered on 16-frame, 224 by 224 clips inherited from VideoMAE; longer sequences require overlap alignment or sparse-tracking memory. Training is primarily synthetic and multi-task, with scale-invariant losses and scale-consistency constraints across depth, 3D track depth, and pose, so the outputs can be visualized together but are not shown to recover absolute metric scale. Camera pose and motion segmentation are presented as additional heads/extensions trained and evaluated by the paper, while the main architecture remains a feed-forward video encoder with lightweight dense and sparse task heads.
