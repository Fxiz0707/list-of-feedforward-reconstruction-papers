**Paper:** SpatialTrackerV2: 3D Point Tracking Made Easy, arXiv Jul 2025, SpatialTrackerV2
**Summary:** SpatialTrackerV2 is a feed-forward 3D point tracking method for monocular RGB video that jointly estimates consistent per-frame video depth, camera poses (extrinsics + focal length), 2D/3D point trajectories, visibility scores, and dynamic probabilities for queried points in a single unified, fully differentiable pipeline. It comprises a temporal video depth and camera-pose front end plus a SyncFormer iterative back end that decouples and refines ego-motion and object motion; it is trained end-to-end on 17 heterogeneous datasets spanning synthetic sequences, posed RGB-D video, pose-only data, and unlabeled in-the-wild footage. No per-scene optimization is performed at inference.

1. **3D reconstruction:** Yes. The model produces a dense 3D scene representation: the front end generates per-frame metric-aligned depth maps that, combined with predicted camera poses, form fused point clouds (Fig. 2 "Initial Point Cloud," Fig. 5 "Fused Point Clouds"). The paper evaluates this as a dynamic 3D reconstruction system directly against MegaSAM (Sec. 4.2, pp. 6–7).
2. **Dense reconstruction:** Yes. Dense reconstruction is inherent to the method: the front-end video depth estimator (based on DepthAnythingV2 + temporal alternating attention) produces per-pixel depth for every frame, which is used both for 3D tracking initialization and evaluated as a standalone dense output in Sec. 4.2.1 (Table 2, pp. 6–7).
3. **World-space output:** Yes. The back end explicitly registers 3D tracks into a world coordinate frame via weighted Procrustes analysis (Sec. 3.2: "register T^{3d}_{k+1} into the world coordinate frame"), producing T^{world}_{k+1}. Fig. 5 visualizes "long-term 3D point trajectories in world space" (p. 6).
4. **Camera-space output (egocentric):** Yes. SyncFormer maintains and updates 3D trajectories T^{3d} in the per-frame camera coordinate space as its working 3D representation (Sec. 3.2: "the camera coordinate space for 3D trajectories," p. 4). 3D correlations are computed on normalized camera-space point maps.
5. **Flexible camera frame:** No. Camera-space output exists (Q4 = Yes), but the reference frame is always the current frame's estimated camera coordinate system; the paper describes no mechanism to freely select an arbitrary reference camera frame (Sec. 3.2, p. 4).
6. **Metric scale:** Yes. The front end applies an explicit scale-shift estimation module to produce scale-aligned depth consistent with metric camera translations (Sec. 3.1: "estimate scale-aligned camera trajectories and video depth"). The model is trained with metric RGB-D depth and metric pose supervision; the camera tracker decodes absolute translation vectors. Evaluation protocols that apply scale-and-shift depth alignment (Table 2) or Sim(3) pose alignment (Table 3) are standard normalization steps for fair cross-method comparison and do not indicate the model's outputs are inherently up-to-scale only.
7. **Point tracking:** Yes. Point tracking is the primary task: given N query pixel locations in frame 1, the method recovers long-term 2D and 3D trajectories for each query across all T frames (Sec. 3, p. 3).
8. **3D tracks:** Yes. The paper explicitly defines its goal as recovering "pixel-wise 3D trajectories T = (T^t_i)^{t=1..T}_{i=1..N}" and SyncFormer outputs T^{3d} ∈ R^{T×N×3} in camera coordinates (Sec. 3, Sec. 3.2, pp. 3–4).
9. **2D tracks:** Yes. SyncFormer simultaneously outputs 2D trajectories T^{2d} ∈ R^{T×N×2} in UV space (Sec. 3.2, Eq. 3). 2D tracking is quantitatively evaluated on TAP-Vid DAVIS (Table 4, Sec. 4.3, p. 8).
10. **Occlusion / visibility prediction:** Yes. SyncFormer outputs per-trajectory visibility probability p^{vis} (Eq. 3, Sec. 3.2, p. 4). Occlusion Accuracy (OA) is reported for every subset in Table 1 (p. 5).
11. **Multiple views:** No. The input is a single monocular video. The abstract explicitly states "feed-forward 3D point tracking method for monocular videos," and Sec. 3 defines the input as a T-frame video from a single camera (p. 1, 3).
12. **Global context vs. pairwise vs. fixed-window:** Global. The temporal encoder uses alternating intra-frame self-attention and inter-frame attention over all flattened video tokens (Sec. 3.1, p. 3), and SyncFormer updates trajectories and poses across the full sequence at each iteration. Training uses finite-length video windows (1–24 frames in Stage 2, 12–48 in Stage 3), but the architecture processes whatever full sequence is provided without a sliding window at inference.
13. **No GT extrinsics required at inference:** Yes. The Neural Camera Tracker predicts camera poses from input video features with no ground-truth extrinsics supplied at inference (Sec. 3.1, Eq. 1, p. 3). The back end then refines those poses via differentiable bundle adjustment.
14. **Extrinsics as output:** Yes. The camera head H decodes per-frame pose parameters P^t ∈ R^{T×8} (quaternion rotation + translation vector, Eq. 1), and camera pose estimation is quantitatively evaluated against ground truth on TUM-dynamic, Lightspeed, and Sintel (Table 3, Sec. 4.2.2, p. 7).
15. **No GT intrinsics required at inference:** Yes. The camera encoding P^t includes a predicted normalized focal length; no ground-truth intrinsics are required as input (Sec. 3.1, Eq. 1, p. 3).
16. **Intrinsics as output:** Partial. The model predicts normalized focal length as part of the 8-dimensional camera parameter vector (Eq. 1, p. 3), but the output does not include a full intrinsic matrix (no principal point or other distortion parameters are described).
17. **Moving cameras:** Yes. Ego camera motion is a first-class modeled component (Sec. 3.1 "Ego Motion Component"). The method estimates camera trajectories and is evaluated on datasets with strong ego-motion: TAPVid-3D Aria (egocentric), TUM-dynamic, Lightspeed, and Sintel (Sec. 4.1, 4.2.2, pp. 5–7).
18. **Dynamic content:** Yes. Object motion T_object is explicitly modeled as a separate component from ego-motion (Sec. 3). The back end predicts per-point dynamic probability p^{dyn}, and training data includes dynamic segmentation supervision and dynamic tracking annotations (Sec. 3.2, Sec. 3.3, pp. 4–5). The method is evaluated on the dynamic DriveTrack subset and TUM Dynamics benchmark.
19. **Feed-forward inference:** Yes. The paper repeatedly describes SpatialTrackerV2 as "feed-forward" and explicitly contrasts it with per-scene optimization methods (Abstract, p. 1). The differentiable bundle adjustment is used only during training as a learning signal; the SyncFormer's iterative refinement at inference is a fixed-depth recurrent forward pass (analogous to RAFT iterations), not test-time optimization. Inference takes 5–10 seconds for a 100-frame video versus 5–10 minutes for MegaSAM (Sec. 4.2.1, p. 7).

**Notes:** Input modality is monocular RGB video only; no depth sensor, stereo, or multi-camera rig is required. Query points for tracking are 2D pixel coordinates specified in the first frame. Intrinsic output is limited to focal length (one scalar per frame); full calibration including principal point is not estimated. The metric-scale claim is strongest when training-time metric supervision is applicable; for purely unlabeled footage the model uses a monocular depth teacher with a self-supervised scale-consistency objective. Training spans 17 datasets with heterogeneous supervision types requiring a 3-stage curriculum. Downstream applications highlighted include robotics, video generation, and 4D reconstruction.
